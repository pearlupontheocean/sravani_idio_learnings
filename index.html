Flow:

In the class, we discussed about flow. The concept of Flow, as defined by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, offers profound insights into the psychology of optimal performance. It encapsulates the experience of being fully immersed in an activity, resulting in heightened focus and an altered sense of time. I believe we are fully at ease and fully present when we are in the flow state.

But I dont think achieving the flow state fully is practically possible in chaotic workplace settings.

I feel that while Flow emphasizes individual experience, it often overlooks social and collective dimensions of engagement, which are critical in team-oriented settings.

A video of Mohini Dey, a musician was shown. She was so engrossed in playing her instrument that she was oblivious to her environment. The act and her became one thing. A state of flow entails the following characteristics:

  1. Focus
  2. Timelessness
  3. The state of being one with the act

Being a singer myself, the concept of flow was very near to heart and I felt that I understood the concept intuitively rather than theoretically. Once I start singing, I forget about my immediate surroundings because there is a more important task at hand to deliver a good performance. But once I start focusing on the notes, I forget about the performance itself.

What is my personality type?

Another day of excitement continued when we were introduced to the concept of Personality.

IDIO classes are always something to look forward because each class incorporates a new way of teaching.

The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) is a well-known personality assessment tool.

It evaluates personality based on four dichotomies:

    • Introversion (I) vs. Extraversion (E)
    • Sensing (S) vs. Intuition (N)
    • Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F)
    • Judging (J) vs. Perceiving (P)

The MBTI was first developed in the 1940s by Isabel Briggs Myers and Katharine Cook Briggs, inspired by Carl Jungs theory of psychological types.

Ikigai: Japanese secret to long and happy life

Ikigai is a Japanese concept that translates to "a reason for being" or "a reason to wake up in the morning." It is often depicted as the intersection of four core elements:

  1. What You Love (Your Passion): Things you truly enjoy and are passionate about.
  2. What You Are Good At (Your Vocation): Skills and talents that you excel in or are capable of mastering.
  3. What the World Needs (Your Mission): How your actions, talents, or contributions help society or make the world a better place.
  4. What You Can Be Paid For (Your Profession): The ability to make a living from what you do.

When all these elements align, the result is a fulfilling and meaningful life, where work, personal values, and societal needs intersect. Ikigai is not just about professional success but also personal happiness and societal contribution. In Japanese culture, its often seen as a source of purpose that leads to greater satisfaction and longevity.

For individuals, it is about finding that sweet spot where personal fulfillment meets external impact, leading to a sense of balance and satisfaction.

 

I strongly believe that finding my ikigai in a professional setting will foster greater success by aligning passion with financial reward. This alignment leads to increased motivation and a strong sense of purpose, driving greater productivity .

Intrinsic motivation: Becoming a self-actualizing individual:

Self-Actualization and Motivation

Self-actualization is a concept rooted in psychology, famously introduced by Abraham Maslow in his Hierarchy of Needs. It represents the highest level of psychological development, where individuals strive to realize their full potential, talents, and capabilities. Self-actualization is often seen as the ultimate goal of personal growth, encompassing creativity, fulfillment, and the pursuit of meaningful goals.

The Role of Motivation in Self-Actualization

Motivation is the driving force behind human behavior and plays a pivotal role in achieving self-actualization. According to Maslow's theory, motivation operates on a hierarchy, starting with basic physiological needs (e.g., food, water) and progressing to safety, love and belonging, esteem, and finally self-actualization. This progression highlights that self-actualization is achieved only when more fundamental needs are met.

 

An important theory here is the Cognitive Evaluation Theory of Motivation. It is a theory in psychology that is designed to explain the effects of external consequences on internal motivation. The theory suggests that there are two motivation systems; intrinsic and extrinsic which correspond to two kinds of motivators. Intrinsic motivators can be achievement, responsibility, and competence motivators that come from the actual performance of the task or job. Extrinsic motivators can be pay, promotion, feedback, and working conditions things that come from a persons environment, controlled by others.

Systems Thinking:

System 1 and System 2 represent two modes of thinking. System 1 is fast, intuitive, and automatic, handling routine decisions effortlessly but prone to biases. System 2 is slow, analytical, and deliberate, used for complex reasoning and problem-solving. Together, they complement each other, balancing efficiency and rationality in decision-making.

Discovering your creative potential guest lecture by Mr.Shiva:

This lecture was very interactive and engaging. We learnt about the 6 thinking hats.

The Six Thinking Hats is a decision-making and problem-solving framework developed by Edward de Bono. It encourages parallel thinking by allowing individuals or groups to focus on different perspectives one at a time, promoting clarity and reducing conflict. Each "hat" represents a distinct way of thinking.

White Hat The Neutral Hat (Facts and Information):

  • Focus: Objective information and data.
  • Purpose: To gather and analyze relevant facts, figures, and known information without interpretation or bias.

Red Hat The Emotional Hat (Feelings and Intuition):

  • Focus: Emotions, feelings, and gut instincts.
  • Purpose: To allow individuals to express their emotions and subjective perceptions without justification or judgment.

Black Hat The Cautious Hat (Judgment and Risks):

  • Focus: Risks, weaknesses, and potential problems.
  • Purpose: To identify challenges, flaws, and why something might not work.

Yellow Hat The Optimistic Hat (Benefits and Positives):

  • Focus: Opportunities, benefits, and positive aspects.
  • Purpose: To explore the upside and find value in an idea or approach.

Green Hat The Creative Hat (Ideas and Alternatives):

  • Focus: Innovation, creativity, and new possibilities.
  • Purpose: To explore alternatives, generate new ideas, and think outside the box.

Blue Hat The Managerial Hat (Process and Control):

  • Focus: Managing the thinking process and ensuring discipline.
  • Purpose: To define goals, set agendas, and maintain focus during discussions.

Benefits of the Six Thinking Hats

  • Encourages balanced thinking by separating emotions from logic.
  • Reduces conflict by fostering collaboration and mutual understanding.
  • Improves creativity by encouraging all participants to step into diverse thinking roles.
  • Enhances problem-solving by addressing challenges from all angles.

I found that the 6 thinking hats technique encourages creativity and innovation, promotes focus and efficiency, enhances decision-making and balances rational and emotional thinking. I find myself using the 6 thinking hats technique sub-consciously when I need to take important decisions.

The case:

The development of the new medical device has encountered several challenges, including high costs and a three-year timeline, compounded by a change in the supplier for the microprocessor. While this new supplier promises a 15% improvement rather than the expected 20%, the product faces ongoing quality issues, particularly concerning faulty internal components and unclear instructions for use. These issues have led to consumer misuse, a major concern highlighted by multiple stakeholders involved in the project.

Kurt, the Director of Consumer Insights, along with Nick and Dr.Greg Young, pointed out that users often struggle with the correct use of the device, especially when it comes to inserting the test strip. Testing conducted on a small sample of just 156 people, most of whom had limited educational backgrounds, showed that many users were initially confused about how to properly use the device. However, when provided with clear instructions, especially from medical practitioners, 55% of the users reported feeling more confident about their ability to use the device correctly.

Dr. Sandy Powers raised concerns that the issues could stem from consumer misuse, particularly among elderly individuals who might not follow the instructions as intended. Nurses also noted improper use, including incorrect insertion of the test strips, further highlighting the need for better guidance.

To address these concerns, Walter Jackson, Chairman of the Board, approved a substantial expenditure of $1 million to ensure the device is used properly. This investment is intended to improve consumer understanding and reduce errors in usage, ensuring the device performs as intended and meets the expected standards of quality and safety. The ultimate goal is to enhance user experience, minimize misuse, and ensure the device fulfills its intended purpose effectively.
